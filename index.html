The Opportunity

A singular resort. A rare entry point.

A 92-key resort on the primary leisure corridor of one of the country’s strongest drive-to markets, entering at roughly half of replacement cost and a fraction of the nearest comparable transaction. This is what makes the deal: not the story, the basis.

Heritage

The real story

Before the numbers, there is the story. This is the part that cannot be underwritten, replicated, or bought back into existence. Tiki was never just about the alcohol. It was always about the fantasy. About building a world vivid enough that the one outside disappeared.

In 1964, hotelier Ken Kimes opened The Tropics as the crown jewel of his five-property Polynesian chain, the most elaborate of them, and the only one that still survives. He commissioned Oceanic Arts of Whittier, California, the firm behind Disney’s Enchanted Tiki Room, Walt Disney World’s Polynesian Village Resort, Trader Sam’s, Trader Vic’s, and Don the Beachcomber, to carve the property’s tikis. Artist Ed Crissman carved 24 of them onsite from palm trunks. Oceanic Arts closed permanently in 2021 after sixty-five years. What remains at The Tropics cannot be ordered again.

This was never about accuracy. It was about escape. Paradise, in places like this, is a design decision.

The Tropics became the south-end answer to the Racquet Club, a place where Palm Springs glamour gave way to something more transportive, more intimate, more theatrical. The Rat Pack drank here. Elvis Presley and Nancy Sinatra were regulars at the pool. Victor Mature kept a reserved table in the Congo Room steakhouse. The underground Cellar bar seated one hundred. Professional baseball teams stayed during spring training. For two decades, this was where Southern California’s showbusiness class came to disappear.

The best tropical places feel invented and inevitable at once. That is what The Tropics still holds. Not just a style, but a fully authored world.

In August 2001, The Tropics became the birthplace of Tiki Oasis, now the largest tiki event in the world. The festival remained at the property until it outgrew the venue in 2005 and moved to San Diego. Tiki Caliente, the property’s own annual festival, still fills the grounds each year. More than sixty years after opening, the original 1964 A-frame entrance still stands. The 100,000-gallon pool retains its original configuration. The tikis remain where Ed Crissman left them.

Tiki works when the world outside disappears. If you can’t get to paradise, this brings it to you. That is the real asset here.

Land

The land underneath the deal

The Tropics sits on Agua Caliente Band of Cahuilla Indians tribal trust land under a 99-year ground lease administered through the Bureau of Indian Affairs. This is not incidental. It’s how you acquire frontage on this stretch of Palm Canyon Drive at all. Fee-simple dirt on this corridor effectively doesn’t trade.

Structure

Lessor: Agua Caliente Band of Cahuilla Indians
Administered through: Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA), U.S. Department of the Interior
Term: 99-year ground lease
Basis treatment: Ground rent expensed through NOI; leasehold improvements amortized through FF&E reserve and capex plan

Why It’s a Feature, Not a Bug

Ground leases on Agua Caliente land underlie a substantial share of Palm Springs’ hotel, resort, and commercial inventory, including a number of the comparables referenced elsewhere in this deck. The structure is mature, lender-familiar, and routinely financed by senior commercial lenders and the SBA. The tribe is a long-tenured, institutional counterparty with a demonstrated track record of supporting hospitality use on trust land.

What Investors Should Know

On residual value. The exit valuation ($23.8M at 8.5% cap) reflects leasehold economics consistent with market-comparable Palm Springs hotel transactions, the majority of which involve BIA-administered leasehold interests. The leasehold cap rate is the market cap rate for this corridor.

On financing. Both the senior mortgage (50%) and SBA (30%) are structured with leasehold collateral. BIA consent processes for financing, refinance, and eventual sale are well-established and accounted for in the transaction timeline.

Under diligence. Remaining lease term, ground rent schedule and escalator mechanics, BIA assignment and consent procedures, and lender-side lease-review conditions are under review as part of closing diligence. LBHG’s 30 years of Coachella Valley operating experience, including active operations on comparable ground-lease structures, informs this underwriting.

Operating Upside Not Included in Base Case

The base case underwrites market-standard operations. Not included in these numbers: AI-driven revenue management systems (peer boutiques see 3–6% RevPAR uplift vs. manual pricing), dynamic F&B yield management during compression events, and direct-booking optimization that shifts share away from OTA commissions. A 5% RevPAR uplift at stabilization would add approximately $270K in annual revenue on 33,580 room-nights; moving 5–10% of bookings from OTA to direct would add another ~$40–80K annually to NOI. These are operating levers LBHG intends to pursue; they are treated as upside optionality rather than built into the underwritten returns.

Oceanic Arts Provenance
Tiki gods and artifacts throughout the resort were designed and manufactured by Oceanic Arts (Whittier, CA), founded 1956 by LeRoy Schmaltz and Bob Van Oosting. Oceanic Arts also created decor for Disney’s Enchanted Tiki Room, Trader Sam’s at Disneyland, Walt Disney World’s Polynesian Village Resort, Trader Vic’s, and Don the Beachcomber. Artist Ed Crissman carved 24 tikis from palm trunks onsite at The Tropics. Oceanic Arts closed permanently on November 24, 2021 after 65 years.

Ace Hotel Transaction & Basis Premium
Ace Hotel & Swim Club Palm Springs acquired by JRK Property Holdings from GFI Capital in October 2022 for ~$60 million per CoStar data ($335K per key on 179 rooms). Transaction terms undisclosed by seller; $60M figure reported by CoStar and cited in Ace Hotel CEO succession coverage (Jan 2025). JRK publicly stated intent to invest “significant strategic capital.” JRK’s adjacent Austin 602-room Marriott portfolio received ~$40K+/key renovation spend. Applying a comparable figure to Ace implies an all-in basis approaching $445K/key, making The Tropics’ $157K/key all-in approximately 65% below the Ace estimated all-in. Renovation figure is directional; exact Ace capex not publicly disclosed.
Source: CoStar; Hotel News Now Ace CEO coverage (Jan 10, 2025); Hotel-Online press releases (Oct 20, 2022); Hotel Investment Today (JRK Puerto Rico coverage, Feb 2024); Bisnow Los Angeles Deal Sheet
